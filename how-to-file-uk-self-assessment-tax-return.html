How to File a UK Self Assessment Tax Return: Step-by-Step Guide (2026)

The Digital Park · Updated June 2026 · Reading time: 9 minutes

If you are self employed in the UK, the self assessment tax return is the bit nobody warns you about until it is nearly due. The good news is that filing it is far simpler than HMRC makes it look, once you understand the order things happen in. This guide walks you through the whole process step by step, in plain English, so you can file with confidence and stop losing sleep over it.

By the end you will know exactly what to gather, how to register, which expenses you can claim, and the dates that actually matter. We will keep it human and practical. No jargon dumps.

This guide is general information, not personal tax advice. For anything unusual in your situation, speak to a qualified accountant.

Who has to file a self assessment tax return?

You usually need to file if any of the following applied in the tax year (6 April to 5 April):

  • You were self employed as a sole trader and earned more than the trading allowance threshold.
  • You earned untaxed income from things like freelancing, a side hustle, renting out a property, or dividends.
  • You were a partner in a business partnership.
  • You needed to pay the High Income Child Benefit Charge or had other income HMRC could not collect through PAYE.

If you are working out self employed tax in the UK for the first time, this is the moment people tend to panic. Do not. The form is long but most boxes will not apply to you.

The deadlines that actually matter

Missing a deadline triggers automatic penalties, so put these in your calendar now:

  • 5 October after the tax year ends: deadline to register for self assessment if it is your first year.
  • 31 October: deadline for a paper return (most people skip this and file online).
  • 31 January: deadline to file your online return AND pay the tax you owe.
  • 31 July: second payment on account due, if you make payments on account.

Step 1: Register for self assessment with HMRC

If this is your first self assessment tax return, register on the gov.uk website. HMRC will post you a Unique Taxpayer Reference (UTR) number. This can take up to ten working days, so do not leave registration to January.

  1. Go to the official gov.uk self assessment registration page.
  2. Choose the option that matches you (sole trader, not self employed but with other income, or partner).
  3. Wait for your UTR to arrive by post, then set up your online Government Gateway account.

Step 2: Gather your income records

Before you open the form, pull together every source of income for the tax year. For most self employed people that means:

  • Total sales or fees invoiced and received.
  • Any employment income (your P60 or P45) if you also had a job.
  • Interest, dividends, rental income, or other untaxed income.

Having a single tidy figure for your turnover here saves you hours. This is exactly where a simple income tracker earns its keep.

Step 3: Total up your allowable expenses

This is the step that saves you money, and the step most people rush. You only pay tax on your profit, which is income minus allowable business expenses. Common allowable expenses for the self employed include:

  • Office costs (stationery, phone, software subscriptions).
  • Travel for work (mileage or public transport, not your commute).
  • A proportion of home working costs if you work from home.
  • Professional fees, insurance, and training that maintains your existing skills.
  • Stock and materials.
Common mistake: claiming things that are not allowable (like client entertaining or your daily commute) or, more often, forgetting to claim things you are entitled to and paying more tax than you need to.

Step 4: Work out your profit

Profit is simply total income minus total allowable expenses. Keep the two figures separate and clearly labelled so you can drop them straight into the relevant boxes on the form. If your numbers live in one organised spreadsheet, this step takes minutes instead of an afternoon.

Step 5: Complete and submit the online return

  1. Log in to your Government Gateway account and start your self assessment.
  2. Fill in the self employment section with your turnover, expenses, and profit.
  3. Add any other income (employment, interest, dividends, property).
  4. Review the calculated tax owed before you submit. HMRC shows you the figure.
  5. Submit, then note your submission reference.

Step 6: Pay what you owe by 31 January

Filing and paying are two separate actions, both due by 31 January. Pay via the gov.uk payment options. If your bill is over a certain threshold you may also need to make a payment on account towards next year, so check the figure HMRC gives you carefully.

Your self assessment checklist

  • Registered for self assessment and have your UTR
  • Government Gateway account set up
  • Total income for the tax year recorded
  • All allowable expenses totalled
  • Profit calculated (income minus expenses)
  • Other income added (employment, interest, dividends, rental)
  • Return filed online before 31 January
  • Tax paid before 31 January

Frequently asked questions

Do I need an accountant to file a self assessment tax return?

No. Many self employed people file their own return online without an accountant, especially when their affairs are straightforward. A clear checklist and an organised record of income and expenses is usually all you need.

What happens if I miss the 31 January deadline?

HMRC issues an automatic penalty as soon as the deadline passes, with further penalties and interest the longer you leave it. File on time even if you cannot pay the full amount immediately, then arrange payment.

Can I claim expenses from previous years?

Expenses belong to the tax year in which they were incurred. Keep records for each year separately so nothing slips through.
